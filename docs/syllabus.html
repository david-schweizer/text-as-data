Course description


Against the background of the ever-increasing availability of (unstructured) data, the goal of this seminar is to equip you with the necessary skills to collect large quantities of text, to process it, and to analyse it. These skills allow you to pursue your own empirical research projects. The course consists of three major modules and is a very practical introduction to computational text analysis with the R programming language.

  • First, you will be guided into the programming language learning the fundamentals of R and get to know the workflow for data analysis: Import, tidy, transform, and visualize data. No prior experience with R is necessary!

  • Second, we will learn how to implement various text analysis methods and understand the intuition behind these methods.

  • Third, we will cover static and dynamic web scraping and use these tools to collect our own text data.

The goal of this course is to equip you with the skills to:

  • collect your own data

  • tidy, transform & visualize the data

  • analyze the data & communicate your results

  • collaborate with others along the workflow

Taking this course should not be an end in itself. The motivation for this course is therefore twofold: First, these skills prepare you for conducting empirical research (including term papers, BA or MA theses), collaborating with other students / researchers, and communicating your work. Second, mastering these skills also prepare you for a career outside academia.

There are no prerequisites for this course, except the willingness to familiarise yourself with the R programming language.

Term paper (100% of your grade):

In a nutshell: Ask a question you’re curious about, collect relevant data and analyze them with regard to your question using the techniques learned in this seminar.

The term paper should include the following:

  • Brief introduction and research question: Here, you should introduce the topic you are addressing, your research question, and the relevance of your contribution.

  • Expectations / hypotheses: This should include a concise overview of your theoretical expectation(s) that you are testing. What literature do you build on /relate to? A handful of journal articles is enough.

  • Data & Methods: In this section, you should describe the data source(s). Here, you should also discuss the scope of your study (e.g., case selection). Descriptive statistics of the corpus might be helpful (e.g., speeches: How many speeches delivered overall, how many by party, …). In addition, you should describe the methods you are using: How do they help to answer the research question? You might want to discuss advantages/disadvantages compared to other approaches.

  • Results: Here, you can present (descriptive) empirical evidence using figures or tables.

  • Conclusion: A brief evaluation of your results and your contribution (to to literature). What can we learn from the term paper? What might be limitations?

  • Each term paper has to be accompanied by a statutory declaration in which you assure your independent working. The wording of such a declaration can be found in the actually valid examination order PO: “Hiermit versichere ich, dass diese Arbeit von mir persönlich verfasst ist und dass ich keinerlei fremde Hilfe in Anspruch genommen habe. Ebenso versichere ich, dass diese Arbeit oder Teile daraus weder von mir selbst noch von anderen als Leistungsnachweise andernorts eingereicht wurden. Wörtliche oder sinngemäße Übernahmen aus anderen Schriften und Veröffentlichungen in gedruckter oder elektronischer Form sind gekennzeichnet. Overall, the term paper should contain 3000 - 3500 words excluding title page, references, and appendix. Please stick to this word limit! The title page should at least include your term paper’s title, your name, the name of this seminar, the semester, as well as the date of submission.

In addition to the text, you should submit your R code. The script should be neatly presented and annotated. Do not include all code chunks that you tested but only the ones that you use for your final version of the term paper.

In any case, I recommend to discuss your approach and ideas either via e-mail or during office hours!

A general breakdown of scoring is as follows:

  • 1,0: Outstanding effort.

  • 1,3 - 2,0: Good effort.

  • 2,3 - 3,0: Good/Passing effort.

  • 3,3 - 4,0: Struggling effort.

  • Fail.

Assessment will be based on several criteria. Given the methodological focus of this seminar, particular emphasis will be placed on the quality and rigor of data selection, analytical methods, and implementation. An overview of the most important grading components is provided below. The grading related both to content but also takes into account structure and formal components.

Research question Clear, focused, and relevant research question in the area of political science and text analysis.
Data & Preprocessing Appropriate text corpus for the research question, correct data cleaning and pre-processing? Justify your steps.
Methodology Appropriate use of text analysis method(s) with regard to your research question. Explain why you use a particular approach (e.g., a dictionary approach vs. a topic modeling approach).
Results Correct interpretation of results? Godd use of figures or tables? Are the results linked back to the research question?
Conclusion Key takeaway? Possible limitations adressed?
Structure & Clarity Logical flow? Clear writing? Consistent terminology? Word count? Consistent citation style throughout the paper (your choice) and correct bibliography (e.g., all citations in the text also in the bibliography)?
Code & Reproducibility Clean and annotated code? Replication of analysis possible?
Bonus: Originality / Creativity / Effort Original, creative research with regards to data and methods? Effort?
